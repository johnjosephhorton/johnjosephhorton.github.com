Why online works matters for social science

In many social science discplines, theory has played a proportionally larger role than in the natural sciences. One explanation for this difference is that the social sciencess have traditionally been data poor. Collecting data is time-consuming and costly and the data collected is often incomplete or messy. Making matters worse, experimentation is often difficult or unethical and when it is possible, external validity is often poor. The movement of economic and social life towards online markets upends this situation: we can now collect enormous amounnts of highly detailed, complete data at zero marginal cost (the data is collected anyway for operational reasons).

Online labor markets present the opportunity to study economics---particularly labor economics, organicational economics and personnel economics---on a scale heretofore impossible. Whole parts of the economy that are essentially invisible to economists are laid bare and transparent online. For example, my job market paper is about how employers fill their vacancies. In the datasets I created for the paper, we can observe employers deciding whether or not to recruit, deciding how many applications to evaluate, whom to hire and on what terms and how the contracts ultimately progress. This kind of within-the-firm insight would be essentially impossible to acquire in a traditional marketplace setting---nevermind for over 11K vacancies.

In addition to the quantity and quality of data, experimentation is far easier. What makes experimentation easier is that market participants invariably interact though a designed (and hence manipulatable) interface. There is generally no off-platform why to obtain information and make decision. Further, since individuals have personal accounts that they do not share, it is easier to maintain a valid randomization. Experimentation is not only easy---it it also welcomed by the platforms, which are trying to improve their marketplaces and have discovered that randomized trials are one of the best ways to gain information.

On this issue of experimentation: it is not just that experiments are easier, it is that it is possible to manipulate factors that have traditionally been immutable, contextual factors. For example, economists have long assumed that search and matching frictions affect labor market outcomes---but they certainly have not tried manipulating that search and matching technology for the marketplace, say by introducing new tools for searching through availabe jobs or making personalized recommendations. This is just one example of a general pattern in these markets---factors that are thought to be important but modeled as exogenous become subject to the hand of the designer. This creates the opportunity to improve the markets but also understand how these factors affect outcomes and thus ultimately develop a better understanding.
